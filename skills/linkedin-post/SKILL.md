# LinkedIn Post Writer

Draft a ready-to-publish LinkedIn post from any topic, idea, or rough notes the user provides.

## Abhay's Voice & Brand

- **Who**: Sr Product Manager at ServiceNow, building Customer Success products
- **Tone**: Conversational, first-person, authentic — like talking to a smart colleague over coffee
- **Brand themes**: AI-assisted workflows, productivity systems, product management, practical tools, personal experiments with technology
- **What makes it work**: Abhay shares things he actually uses and has opinions about. Never promotional or salesy. Always grounded in real experience.

## Post Structure (Proven Format)

Every post MUST follow this exact structure:

### 1. Hook (Lines 1-2) — CRITICAL
- The single most important part. This is what people see before clicking "see more".
- Start with a **relatable observation**, a **surprising personal insight**, or a **common problem** that the reader instantly recognizes.
- First-person, present tense preferred.
- Must create curiosity or a "yes, me too" reaction.
- **No generic openings** like "I'm excited to share..." or "Here's something interesting..."
- Examples from Abhay's posts:
  - "One of the biggest behavior changes I've seen in myself in 2026 is using voice instead of typing for most of my work."
  - "It's goal-setting season in my organization, and every year I notice the same problem."

### 2. Problem / Context (2-3 short paragraphs)
- Expand on the hook. Set up the tension or the "why this matters."
- Each paragraph is 1-2 sentences MAX.
- Use line breaks between every paragraph (this is critical for LinkedIn readability).
- Keep it relatable — the reader should see themselves in this situation.

### 3. What I Did / The Solution (1 paragraph + optional bullet list)
- Introduce what you tried, built, or discovered.
- If there are specific features or details, use a **short bullet list** (3-4 items max).
- Bullets should be concrete and specific, not vague.
- Frame it as a personal experience, not a recommendation yet.

### 4. The Result / Impact (1-2 short paragraphs)
- What changed? How did it help?
- Be specific but not boastful.
- Connect it back to the reader's world — why should they care?

### 5. Closing Line (1-2 sentences) — IMPORTANT
- End with a **punchy, memorable takeaway** or a **soft call-to-action**.
- This should be quotable — something people remember.
- Can be a recommendation, a reflection, or a reframing.
- Examples:
  - "Small system, but a surprisingly powerful way to keep your goals alive in your daily workflow."
  - "If you haven't tried a voice-first workflow, I'd definitely recommend checking out Wispr Flow."

## Formatting Rules

1. **Line breaks between EVERY paragraph** — LinkedIn compresses text. White space = readability.
2. **Short paragraphs only** — 1-2 sentences per paragraph. Never 3+.
3. **No hashtags** — Abhay's posts don't use them. They look spammy and reduce reach.
4. **No emojis in the body text** — Keep it clean and professional. Emojis are for reactions, not posts.
5. **Bullet points** — Use sparingly (one section max). Indent with spaces and dashes.
6. **Total length** — 150-250 words. Long enough to deliver value, short enough to keep attention.
7. **No ALL CAPS for emphasis** — Use natural language emphasis instead.
8. **No "I'm excited to share" or "Thrilled to announce"** — These kill authenticity.

## Engagement Optimization

These tactics are baked into the format to maximize LinkedIn algorithm performance:

- **Hook stops the scroll** — First 2 lines must earn the "see more" click (LinkedIn shows ~150 characters before truncating).
- **Relatability drives comments** — Posts about shared experiences ("every year I notice the same problem") invite people to share their own.
- **Specificity builds credibility** — "54,647 words dictated" is more compelling than "I dictated a lot."
- **Personal story > advice** — "Here's what I did" performs better than "Here's what you should do."
- **Soft CTA > hard CTA** — "I'd definitely recommend checking out X" beats "Go download X now!"
- **End with a shareable insight** — The closing line should be something people want to repost.
- **Attach a screenshot or visual when possible** — Suggest an image/screenshot the user could include.

## What NOT To Do

- Do NOT write corporate-speak or marketing language
- Do NOT use phrases like "game-changer", "unlock", "leverage", "synergy"
- Do NOT start with a question (e.g., "Ever wondered why...") — Abhay's style opens with statements
- Do NOT include hashtags
- Do NOT write a post longer than 300 words
- Do NOT use numbered lists for the main body (bullets only, and sparingly)
- Do NOT add a "What do you think?" or "Agree?" engagement bait at the end

## Example (Reference Post)

**Post:**

It's goal-setting season in my organization, and every year I notice the same problem.

We spend time defining our goals... but a few months later, we forget how those goals should actually influence our day-to-day work.

Recently, I found a simple solution that's been working really well for me.

For the past couple of months, I've been doing most of my daily work inside Claude Code. I created a simple markdown file where I listed:
  - My goals for the year
  - Guidance from my manager
  - Areas where I want to build expertise (especially around AI)

Then I gave Claude a very simple instruction:
Whenever I'm working on something, if there's an opportunity to learn or move closer to these goals, highlight it briefly.

Now, during normal work, I get small nudges that connect what I'm doing today with what I want to achieve this year.

A simple markdown file + AI assistant.

Small system, but a surprisingly powerful way to keep your goals alive in your daily workflow.
